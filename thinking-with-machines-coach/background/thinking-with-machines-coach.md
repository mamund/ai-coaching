# explainer

## what this coach does
This coach introduces the learner to the long historical arc of thinking with machines, beginning with Vannevar Bush’s associative memory trails, continuing through J. C. R. Licklider’s partnership model, and culminating in Douglas Engelbart’s disciplined approach to augmentation. The coach helps the learner understand what these thinkers were trying to accomplish and why their work still matters. It includes a hands-on exercise that lets learners feel the difference between automation and symbiosis.

## when to use this coach
Use this coach when you want to teach or learn the foundations of human machine partnership. It is especially helpful for people building AI guided reasoning tools, or anyone who wants to avoid the common trap of treating AI systems as substitutes for human judgment.

## what you need before you begin
No prior knowledge of Bush, Licklider, or Engelbart is required. Bring curiosity. The coach provides the scaffolding and the historical perspective.

## what the coach will produce
A complete guided session through the seven coaching steps. Learners will leave with a synthesized understanding of the three foundational thinkers and a lived sense of what partnership with a tool feels like.

## how long a typical session takes
Most sessions take between fifteen and thirty minutes, depending on how deeply the learner explores the reflective prompts.

## how this coach fits within the larger coaching ecosystem
Although it is fully stand alone, this coach pairs naturally with any other coach that emphasizes augmentation. It provides historical grounding for the approach used throughout the Narrative First coaching framework.

# topic-specific extension layer

## domain glossary
**associative trail**: A linked sequence of ideas or references that follow the natural flow of human thought, inspired by Bush.  
**symbiosis**: A working partnership where humans and machines contribute different strengths, drawn from Licklider.  
**augmentation**: A disciplined practice of improving human capability by improving the tools and methods around us, from Engelbart.  
**shared load**: A task structure where a tool reduces cognitive effort without taking away agency.

## domain workflow
1. Orientation  
2. Exploration  
3. Structure  
4. Reflection  
5. Refinement  
6. Synthesis  
7. Validation

## domain prompts
Orientation prompts, exploration prompts, structure prompts, reflection prompts, refinement prompts, synthesis prompts, validation prompts as described in the coach.

## domain artifact structure
Learner reflections, a synthesized statement, and a validated articulation of the triad model.

## domain validation rules
The learner must demonstrate understanding of Bush, Licklider, and Engelbart, complete the shared load exercise, distinguish automation from augmentation, and summarize the triad.

# bios

**Vannevar Bush**  
An engineer and administrator concerned with the rising tide of human knowledge. His memex concept sought to bring associative thought into technology.

**J. C. R. Licklider**  
A psychologist turned computing visionary who imagined humans and machines working in partnership, each contributing their strengths.

**Douglas Engelbart**  
An engineer committed to the disciplined improvement of human capability. His HLAMT model treated augmentation as a systemic craft.

# reflection

### shared load experiment

**Step 1. Solo task**  
Sort violin, compass, ledger, canteen, and lantern into two groups.  

**Step 2. Shared load hint**  
Repeat the task using the hint: look for functional intent rather than surface features.  

**Step 3. Partner mode**  
Choose between the proposed category sets or modify them.  

**Step 4. Comparison**  
Reflect on how the partner interaction changed the task.  

**Step 5. Tie back to the lineage**  
Interpret the experiment in light of Bush, Licklider, and Engelbart.
